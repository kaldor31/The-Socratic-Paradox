# Maieutics & The Socratic Paradox

## What is Maieutics?

Maieutics (from the Greek *maieutikos*, "of midwifery") is the Socratic method of eliciting knowledge through disciplined questioning. Rather than delivering answers, the interlocutor asks questions that expose contradictions, clarify assumptions, and guide the thinker toward self-discovered truth.

In *The Socratic Paradox*, this ancient practice is applied to the inner dialogue of the modern mind.

## The Cognitive Layer

Modern cognitive psychology has identified recurring patterns of biased thinking, known as **cognitive distortions**. These distortions are not failures of character; they are habits of mind. Naming them creates the cognitive space required to evaluate them.

Common distortions include:

- **Catastrophizing** — Predicting the worst possible outcome.
- **Mind Reading** — Assuming knowledge of others' thoughts without evidence.
- **All-or-Nothing Thinking** — Seeing situations in black-and-white terms.
- **Emotional Reasoning** — Treating feelings as proof of facts.
- **Should Statements** — Imposing rigid rules on oneself or others.

## The Socratic Loop

Each journal entry is a structured loop:

1. **Thesis** — Capture the raw thought as it arrives.
2. **Interrogation** — Answer Socratic prompts that challenge assumptions.
3. **Distortion Tagging** — Identify which cognitive distortions are present.
4. **Rational Synthesis** — Restate the thought in a balanced, evidence-based form.

The goal is not to feel better; it is to think more clearly.
